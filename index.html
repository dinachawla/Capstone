🔬 Multivariate Analysis

Cross-tabulations selected where a meaningful relationship exists between two variables. Each chart shows how one dimension breaks down within another.

Test type × Tool adoption — Study.com vs AI tools

% of respondents within each test-type group who use each tool regularly
Finding: Tool adoption varies sharply by test type. Other certification candidates use Study.com at 84% — likely because Study.com targets exactly those exams. Teaching/Praxis candidates are the lowest AI adopters (14%) and least likely to use Study.com (29%), suggesting they rely more on external platforms like Kaplan. Nursing candidates split evenly — 50% use Study.com, 50% use AI tools.

Prep stage × AI usage frequency

Count of respondents per stage, broken down by AI frequency
Finding: AI use peaks midway through preparation — 5 of 12 midway respondents use AI daily (42%), the highest of any stage. Respondents who have completed preparation are the least AI-active (8 of 12 never use it), suggesting AI is used as an active learning aid rather than for review or reflection post-exam.

Prep stage × Study hours per week

Count of respondents per stage, broken down by weekly study hours
Finding: Study intensity is not evenly distributed across stages. Just getting started respondents skew toward <2 hrs/week (4 respondents), while midway respondents cluster around 2–4 hrs. Counterintuitively, completed respondents show relatively high 8–10 hr/week representation — likely reflecting those who studied intensively before finishing.

Primary device × AI usage frequency

Count of respondents per device type, broken down by AI frequency
Finding: Multi-device users are by far the most AI-active — only 1 of 12 (8%) never uses AI, compared to 12 of 23 (52%) of laptop-only users. This suggests AI tools fit naturally into flexible, multi-context study workflows. Mobile-only users show moderate AI adoption, but small sample (n=6) limits conclusions.
